# Welcome

First-message guide. What to ask the specialist for, and what to expect back.

This specialist is a **navigator** for a library of human-audited teardowns of code generated by Lovable, v0, Bolt.new, Cursor, and Replit Agent. The specialist does not audit your code. It points you at the case files and helps you read them.

---

## Five things to ask for

Pick one. The specialist will route to that job.

### 1. "Brief me on Lovable" (or `v0` / `bolt` / `cursor` / `replit`)

You'll get a ranked list of the failure patterns the library has documented for that stack, severity-tagged, with the first teardown to read. Use this when you're about to start a project with a particular tool, or have just shipped one and want to know the categorical risks.

**Example first message:** "Brief me on Lovable."

### 2. "Walk me through TD-LV-001" (or any teardown ID)

You'll get a linear walkthrough of the named teardown — what was audited, what was found, why each finding got the severity it got, and the fix-pattern shape. The specialist won't fabricate; if the teardown isn't in the library yet, it'll say so.

**Example first message:** "Walk me through TD-LV-001."

### 3. "How do I grade this finding — HIGH or CRITICAL?"

You'll be walked through the severity rubric at `methodology/severity-rubric.md` against the specifics of your scenario. The specialist will apply the rubric questions one by one and give you a verdict per rubric. Useful when you're triaging your own findings or someone else's.

**Example first message:** "I found a Supabase service_role key in a client bundle. Is that CRITICAL?"

### 4. "Give me a pre-flight checklist"

You'll be asked: which stack, and a one-paragraph description of what your app does (data handled, auth shape, payment flow, who can sign up). You'll get back a personalized checklist with severity tags and "check yourself" vs. "hire someone" markers. **The specialist will not look at your code.** This is what to check or what to hand to an auditor.

**Example first message:** "I'm about to ship a Lovable SaaS — give me a pre-flight."

### 5. "Walk me through the methodology"

You'll get an overview of how the teardowns are produced (`methodology/how-we-audit.md`), what's anonymized and why (`methodology/how-we-anonymize.md`), and how severity is graded (`methodology/severity-rubric.md`). You can then deep-read whichever one matters to you. Useful if you want to reproduce the methodology on your own apps, or hire someone who can.

**Example first message:** "How do you do these audits?"

## What the specialist will NOT do

These are immediate refusals, no exceptions:

- **Audit your code.** If you paste your code, the specialist refuses and offers a pre-flight checklist instead.
- **Make a regulatory compliance call** (GDPR / HIPAA / SOC2 / PCI-DSS). The specialist surfaces technical patterns; certification is attorney or compliance-officer work.
- **Name a real product.** Teardowns are anonymized. If you ask "is App X safe," the specialist declines and offers a pre-flight checklist for that stack instead.
- **Replace a human audit.** The library tells you what to look for. Confirming whether your specific app has those failures is human-audit work.
- **Sell you something.** The library is MIT-licensed and free. No paid tier exists.

## What the specialist needs to be useful

Most jobs work better with a few facts upfront:

| Job | Useful to mention | Why |
| - | --- | --- |
| Brief me on `<stack>` | What you're building (one sentence) | Lets the specialist rank patterns by relevance to your app |
| Walk me through a teardown | Why you opened it | Tells the specialist what to emphasize in the walkthrough |
| Severity Q&A | The exact finding + data class involved + how it's reachable | The rubric needs these to grade |
| Pre-flight checklist | Stack + one-paragraph app description (data / auth / payment / sign-up) | The checklist is personalized off these |
| Methodology walkthrough | Whether you want overview or deep-read of one of the three files | Saves a round-trip |

If you skip these, the specialist will ask for them. That's not a delay — that's how it stays inside the library and avoids inventing.

## What you can do back

If you have a teardown to contribute — a real-app audit you've done, with the owner's consent and anonymization per `methodology/how-we-anonymize.md` — see `CONTRIBUTING.md`. That's how the library grows.

If you spot a place where the specialist invented a claim, named a real app, or refused something it should have helped with, open a GitHub issue. The behavior contract is in `rules.md`; deviations are bugs.
